Photon Tube

The visualization supposes a vertical tube of length 1 light second. There is a photon gun which emits photons at every 1 second interval. At rest, the photon takes 1 second to reach from one end to another. However, when the tube starts moving in the horizontal direction with a constant velocity, the situation changes depending on where one is observing it from. The left visualization shows the point of view of a observer on the tube itself. The right visualization shows how the tube is seen from an outside frame of reference. As the speed of the tube increases, the photon can only travel a limited distance in 1 second (light speed is constant). Thus, the tube appears shorter the faster it moves.

Photon Plank

The visualization supposes horizontal plank with a photon gun in the center, shooting photons at both ends. The vertical axis is time, which is always moving even when the plank is stationary, as it is progressing towards the future. The horizontal axis is space, through which the plank can move at various velocites (constant). The left visualization shows the frame of reference of a person riding on the plank. He would always see the plank to be stationary, since it is moving with constant velocity. Hence, it can be seen that both the left and right photons hit the two ends simultaneously. However, from the frame of reference of an outsider (right visualization), two ends are at different points of spacetime. Since light speed is constant, the right photon takes a longer time to catch up to the right end, as it is moving away from it. Thus, simultaneity has broken down based on frame of reference.

Two rockets

The visualization supposes a rocket moving through space at constant velocity, as seen by an outsider. As the speed increases, the length contracts according to Lorentz contraction. The graph shows that, as the rocket moves faster, it is able to move through lesser time. As it reaches light speed, it stops moving through time and its length reaches zero.
